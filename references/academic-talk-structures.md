# Academic Talk Structures

## 12–15 minute conference talk

A conference talk should prioritize the puzzle, design, one or two main results, and contribution. Recommended structure: title, research puzzle, literature gap, theory/expectations, data/design, measurement or model, main result, mechanism or heterogeneity, robustness/scope note, contribution, closing/Q&A. Do not spend more than two slides on literature.

For empirical sociology and demography, the most common failure is overexplaining literature and underexplaining the empirical comparison. The audience must know what is being compared before they are asked to interpret a coefficient or figure.

## 25–30 minute seminar or internal presentation

A medium-length talk can include a fuller theory section, a more careful design slide, two or three result slides, and a limited robustness section. Recommended structure: title, motivation, puzzle, literature gap, theory, expectations, data/design, measurement, method/model, main result, result extension, mechanism, robustness, limitations, contribution, discussion.

The deck can be more self-contained than a conference talk, but it should still preserve oral pacing. Dense detail belongs in backup unless it is necessary for the live argument.

## 40–45 minute workshop talk

A workshop talk is not just a longer conference talk. It should expose the paper’s architecture and invite feedback. Add slides that explain design choices, alternative explanations, sensitivity checks, and unresolved decisions. Include explicit discussion prompts. Keep backup slides ready for methods and results questions.

For workshop contexts, the deck should help the room diagnose the paper. It is acceptable to include a slide on remaining analytical decisions, alternative theory framing, or uncertainty about scope conditions.

## Dissertation defense

A defense deck needs a broader contribution frame, a clearer methods rationale, and a stronger synthesis. It may contain multiple empirical chapters or studies. Use section slides to mark the dissertation architecture and maintain continuity across chapters.

A defense should show command over the research program, not only the focal paper. Include a final synthesis slide that explains what the dissertation contributes to the field as a whole.

## Job talk

A job talk requires a research agenda frame in addition to the paper. The main paper must remain coherent, but the opening and closing should position the candidate’s broader intellectual contribution. Include backup slides for theory, data, model choices, and robustness.

The closing should connect the focal paper to a larger agenda without turning the paper into a biography. Make the intellectual trajectory legible.

## Teaching or reading-discussion deck

Teaching decks can use the same visual system but should shift from “research claim” to “conceptual scaffolding.” Use section slides, concept comparison slides, evidence/quotation slides, and discussion question slides. Keep the tone academic, but allow more signposting than in a conference talk.
